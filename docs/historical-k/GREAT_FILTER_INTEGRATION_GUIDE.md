## 3. Integration Points in Manuscript

### A. Introduction – Last Paragraph (Framing)

**Location**: End of Introduction, after stating paper's objectives

**Length**: 1 short paragraph

**Suggested Text**:

> Beyond its intrinsic historical interest, K(t) can also be read as a diagnostic for a specific class of "Great Filter" hypotheses (Hanson 1998; Sandberg et al. 2018). Rather than assuming that civilizations end primarily through spectacular technological self-destruction, the "Great Filter of Co-Creative Wisdom" hypothesis suggests that the dominant failure mode is a **gradual collapse of trust, sense-making, and coordination capacity** (Schmachtenberger 2019). Under this view, civilizations that fail to develop the social and ethical capacities to match their technological power do not explode, but **stagnate and go silent**. K(t) can be interpreted as a coarse-grained time series of global coherence that is relevant to this class of hypotheses, though we emphasize that our current work is primarily descriptive and exploratory rather than a direct test of any particular Great Filter mechanism.

**Tone**: Speculative but grounded; acknowledges we're not testing this directly

---

### B. Discussion – "Implications for Civilizational Risk" Subsection

**Location**: Discussion section, after presenting results but before limitations

**Length**: 2-3 paragraphs

**Suggested Text**:

#### Paragraph 1: Set up the Adolescent God framing

> **Implications for the "Adolescent God" phase and civilizational risk.**
> Several authors have argued that technologically mature species enter an "Adolescent God" phase, characterized by an extreme asymmetry between instrumental power and socio-ethical maturity, with artificial intelligence acting as a universal accelerant of both capabilities and vulnerabilities (Bostrom 2014; Tegmark 2017; Schmachtenberger & Wheal 2020). In that regime, the dominant civilizational risk may not be instantaneous extinction from a single catastrophic event, but **long-run degradation of global coordination capacity** — an erosion of the collective ability to make sense of reality, build trust across difference, and coordinate positive-sum solutions to collective action problems.

#### Paragraph 2: Connect to K(t) empirically

> The Great Filter of Co-Creative Wisdom hypothesis models this as a systematic failure to build institutions, norms, and infrastructures capable of sustaining trust and coordination at planetary scale. Our results do not test this hypothesis directly, but they provide a **concrete empirical object for future work**: a provisional, multi-harmonic index of global coherence over time. If the Great Filter is indeed socio-technical rather than purely physical or biological, then tracking whether K(t) continues to rise, saturates, or collapses under increasing technological power becomes a scientifically legible question. Future research could explicitly model scenarios in which information-ecosystem collapse, institutional distrust, or governance failures manifest as structural breaks or secular declines in K(t), and test these against the patterns observed in our historical reconstruction.

#### Paragraph 3: Mention new architectures (brief)

> One implication is that purely local or short-term interventions are unlikely to be sufficient if the failure mode is systemic. Architectures that explicitly aim to increase **verifiable trust and distributed coordination** — such as decentralized identity systems, verifiable credentials, transparent participatory governance protocols, and economic designs that reward long-term cooperation — can be viewed as candidate socio-technical "defenses" whose success or failure might eventually be reflected in K(t). However, we note that any global coordination metric, including K(t), carries the risk of becoming a misused target if adopted prescriptively without careful institutional design (Goodhart 1975). We return to this concern in the ethics section below.

**Tone**: Speculative but empirically grounded; clear this is future work; acknowledges risks

### C. Discussion – "Implications for Economic Design" Subsection (Optional)

**Location**: After civilizational risk section

**Length**: 2 paragraphs

**Suggested Text**:

#### Paragraph 1: K as compass, not target

> **Implications for economic design.**
> If K(t) is even approximately capturing global coherence and co-creative capacity, then a minimally sane economic system should (1) **avoid systematically depressing K(t)** in exchange for narrow short-term gains, and (2) be evaluated in part by whether it supports sustained increases in K over generational timescales. In other words, K(t) is not a replacement for existing economic indicators like GDP or unemployment, but a **complementary diagnostic**: persistent divergence between economic "success" and K (e.g., rising GDP with falling K) would be a strong signal that the system is misaligned with collective flourishing. This suggests that K could function as a **stress test metric** for proposed policies, asking whether a given intervention, when scaled to planetary adoption, would tend to increase or decrease global coordination capacity.

#### Paragraph 2: Avoid Goodhart's Law

> We emphasize that K(t) should **not** be adopted as a direct optimization target. Any metric that becomes a policy objective is subject to Goodhart's Law: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure" (Goodhart 1975). Instead, K(t) is better understood as a **monitoring tool** (analogous to climate indicators like atmospheric CO₂ concentration) that helps societies detect emerging fragmentation or stagnation early, and evaluate whether proposed economic or governance reforms are compatible with long-run coherence. A future research program could ask: what would economic institutions and incentive structures look like if they were designed such that, **when they do what they are "supposed" to do** — allocate resources, coordinate production, foster innovation — the long-run effect is to stabilize or increase K(t), rather than degrade it?

**Tone**: Radical but careful; acknowledges risks; frames K as compass/guardrail not target

### D. Broader Impacts / Ethics Section

**Location**: Near end of Discussion or separate section

**Length**: 1-2 paragraphs

**Suggested Text**:

#### Paragraph 1: Power-wisdom asymmetry + AI as accelerant

> **Ethical considerations and dual-use concerns.**
> Our work is descriptive rather than prescriptive, but it sits against a backdrop in which AI and other exponential technologies are amplifying existing coordination failures. If the primary civilizational risk is a **widening gap between collective power and collective wisdom**, then tools for measuring global coherence over time raise both opportunities and concerns. On the one hand, indices like K(t) could help societies detect emerging fragmentation or stagnation early, and guide the design of institutions that foster co-creative, positive-sum governance. On the other hand, **any global metric can be misused** as a propaganda tool, a mechanism of centralized control, or a justification for coercive policies. The difference between using K(t) to foster genuine coordination and using it to enforce conformity depends entirely on the institutional context and governance structures in which it is embedded.

#### Paragraph 2: Design challenge

> Designing institutions that use such indices to **foster co-creative, positive-sum governance** — rather than to entrench zero-sum competition or authoritarianism — is an open ethical and socio-technical challenge. We believe this challenge is tractable, but it requires explicit attention to transparency, participatory design, pluralism, and resistance to Goodhart's Law. Future work should explore how K(t) or similar coherence metrics could be embedded in governance systems that are **accountable, pluralistic, and designed to resist capture** by narrow interests. We note that this is a prerequisite for any responsible deployment of global coordination metrics in high-stakes policy contexts.

**Tone**: Thoughtful, acknowledges risks, emphasizes design challenge

## 9. Suggested Review Criteria

Before submitting, check that Great Filter integration passes these tests:

1. **Reviewer Compatibility Test**: Would a skeptical empirical reviewer see the Great Filter framing as:
   - ✅ Interesting motivating context
   - ✅ Legitimate future research direction
   - ❌ Speculative overreach that undermines empirical work

2. **Balance Test**: Does the Great Filter content:
   - ✅ Add interesting framing without overwhelming the paper
   - ✅ Connect to existing risk literature (Bostrom, Tegmark)
   - ❌ Take up >10% of the paper's length

3. **Humility Test**: Is it clear that:
   - ✅ K(t) is provisional and exploratory
   - ✅ We're not claiming to have tested Great Filter hypothesis
   - ✅ Future work is needed to validate any civilizational risk claims

4. **Ethics Test**: Have we:
   - ✅ Raised dual-use concerns explicitly
   - ✅ Warned against Goodhart's Law
   - ✅ Emphasized K as monitoring tool, not target

If all four tests pass ✅, the integration is appropriate for the manuscript.
